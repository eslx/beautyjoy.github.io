Our online learning platform   Carefully crafted for adult ELLs

While the edX platform was developed for highly-literate, university students, our work with the platform has confirmed that it can be customized to be made appropriate for low-literacy, Spanish-speaking, adult English language learners. Through participation in edX’s open source initiative (http://code.edx.org/), we have set up our own instance of the platform on which we have full control of the look and feel of the interface, thus enabling us to meet the unique needs of our target learners. More than a simple translation from English into Spanish, we are working towards an interface that will maximize the use of graphical icons rather than the text-heavy navigation that is currently employed. Additionally, we are working towards an interface that will be designed with a far greater emphasis on connecting through mobile devices than the current version which was designed for use on laptops and desktops. The edX team at UC-Berkeley has been quite encouraging with regard to our current and future modifications because of edX’s support of initiatives that increase the internationalization, localization, and mobile access of the platform.

In addition to modifying the platform, we are building courses that make greater use of image, video and audio than is typical of courses currently offered on the platform. The platform’s full compliance with HTML5 means that it can support our move away from text and towards audio, video, and image for all aspects of our courses.

Visit our customized EdX course

While our current instance does not yet fully reflect our future vision, we have already implemented multiple noteworthy modifications that provide an initial glimpse of what can be done on this platform, and the direction in which we are advancing.

  • We have changed the look and feel to reflect our own branding as opposed to that of edX.
  • We have stripped away unnecessary navigation in order to create a more focused interface that fits better on smaller screens.
  • We have developed a curricular sample that makes use of video on every page.
  • We have provided audio versions of all text-based prompts.
  • We have created a sample assessment component that incorporates audio and video.

We are enthusiastic about these modifications, but we see them as just scratching the surface. During the next phase of the project, we will move towards fully graphical navigation while developing a suite of alternative assessment types, and taking full advantage of edX’s seamless integration of Google Hangouts so that our online learners will have access to oral language practice, ideally with volunteer English language informants from the greater community.
